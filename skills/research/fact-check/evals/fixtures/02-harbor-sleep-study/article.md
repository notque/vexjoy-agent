# Short Sleep, Sharp Costs: Harbor Study Tracks Shift Workers

A new Harbor Institute study finds that error rates among short sleepers nearly doubled on attention tasks. The study tracked 480 shift workers over twelve weeks using wrist actigraphy and nightly task batteries, and short-sleep participants averaged 5.2 hours of sleep on work nights.

The takeaway is blunt: sleeping under six hours causes a 22% rise in attention errors.

Lead author Dr. Priya Raman has played down the alarm. "You'd conclude shift work is safe — the raw error rates look modest," Raman said of her own findings in a recent interview.

The study's rigor drew praise: more than 90% of participants completed the full protocol, and after two weeks of longer sleep, participants' error rates returned to baseline. The $2 million project is the institute's largest sleep study to date.
