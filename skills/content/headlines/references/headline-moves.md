# Headline Moves

Ten named moves for Phase 2 volume generation. Each move is one reader reflex. Tag every candidate with its move; cover at least 6 moves per run. Every example assumes a charge already found in Phase 1 — moves shape the charge, they cannot replace it.

## 1. Curiosity gap

Open a specific question the reader can only close by reading. The gap must be real and the payoff must close it — a gap the article never closes is dishonesty, handled by the Phase 3 accuracy check.

- "The One-Line Nginx Default That Took Our API Down"
- "Why the Fastest Marathoner in the Field Finished Last"

Fits: surprising causes, counterintuitive results. Weak when the answer is guessable from the headline.

## 2. Specificity

Carry the concrete detail — number, name, error string, date — into the headline itself. Specifics signal lived experience.

- "CoreDNS Returns NXDOMAIN for Internal Services After Upgrading to 1.31"
- "A 12-Person Bakery Chain Outsold a National Brand in Ohio"

Fits: technical and data-backed material. The default move when the brief is rich.

## 3. Stakes

Name what the reader stands to lose or gain. Cost, risk, or payoff up front.

- "Your Postgres Backups May Be Silently Unrestorable"
- "The Contract Clause Costing Mid-Size Studios Millions"

Fits: readers who own the problem. Weak when the stake is generic ("save time").

## 4. Contrast

Put two true things side by side that should not coexist. The friction is the pull.

- "Local Build Passes, Cloudflare Deploy Fails — Same Commit"
- "Record Revenue, Record Layoffs: Inside the Q3 Report"

Fits: failure-mode stories, before/after, expectation vs. result.

## 5. Question

Ask the question the reader is already asking. The reader's own uncertainty does the work.

- "Is Your Standup Meeting Just a Status Email With Legs?"
- "Can a Promotion Make You Worse at Your Job?"

Fits: shared doubts and debates. Honest only when the article actually answers.

## 6. How-to

Promise a capability the reader wants, named precisely. Outcome in the headline, method in the article.

- "How to Cut Go CLI Startup From 400ms to 40ms"
- "How to Read a Box Score Like a Scout"

Fits: instructional content with a verifiable result. Pair with specificity for strength.

## 7. Number

Use a count or measurement as the spine. Numbers promise structure and bounded effort.

- "Three Config Lines That Halved Our CI Bill"
- "Seven Minutes That Decided the Title Match"

Fits: lists, measured results. Strongest when the number itself surprises.

## 8. Voice-of-reader

Phrase the headline in the words the reader would type or say — their complaint, their search query.

- "I Rotated the API Key and Production Still Worked. That's the Problem."
- "Everyone Says Network, Nobody Says How"

Fits: relatable frustration, search-aligned titles. Weak for formal outlets.

## 9. News peg

Anchor to a current event the reader already cares about, then deliver your charge through it.

- "What the AWS Outage Reveals About Your Single-Region Bet"
- "The Rule Change Behind This Season's Scoring Surge"

Fits: timely material. Requires a real, current peg — a stale peg reads as opportunism.

## 10. Negative space

Lead with what is absent, undone, or refused. The missing thing is the story.

- "The Test Suite Had No Test for the Thing That Broke"
- "No Sponsor, No Coach, No Plan: How She Made the Final"

Fits: omissions, refusals, gaps in conventional practice. Use sparingly — it dulls with repetition.
